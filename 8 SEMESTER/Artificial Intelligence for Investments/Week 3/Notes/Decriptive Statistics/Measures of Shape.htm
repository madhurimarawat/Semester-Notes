Measures of Shape 📈

Properties 📜 Skewness 🔄 Kurtosis ⛰️
Definition 📖 Measure of asymmetry in data distribution. Positive skew means the right tail is longer, negative skew means the left tail is longer. Measure of the peakedness of the data distribution. It indicates whether data has heavy or light tails compared to a normal distribution.
Formula 📝 \(\text{Skewness} = \frac{\sum (x - \mu)^3}{N\sigma^3}\) \(\text{Kurtosis} = \frac{\sum (x - \mu)^4}{N\sigma^4} - 3\)
Use Cases 🎯 Identifies bias in financial returns, test scores, climate data trends Determines if data has outliers, assesses risk in financial modeling
Effect of Outliers ⚠️ Highly affected Highly affected
Real-life Examples 🌍 Income distributions, stock market returns, response times in psychological studies Quality control in manufacturing, earthquake magnitude distributions
Sensitivity to Extreme Values 🚨 Very sensitive Very sensitive
Preferred Scenario ✅ When analyzing data symmetry, detecting trends in economic growth When checking for heavy/light tails, determining risks in insurance claims

Types of Skewness 📊

Type Description Formula Example
Normal Distribution 📈 Mean = Median = Mode
Symmetric bell curve		 \[ S = 0 \] Heights of adults, IQ scores, measurement errors.
Case Study: The heights of people in a large population follow a symmetric normal distribution where most people are around the average height.
Positive Skew 📊 Mean > Median > Mode
Long right tail		 \( S > 0 \)

\[ S = \frac{n}{(n-1)(n-2)} \sum \left( \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{s} \right)^3 \] 		Income distribution, house prices, hospital waiting times.
Case Study: In a city, most people earn between $40,000–$70,000, but a few high earners (CEOs, celebrities) push the mean higher.
Negative Skew 📉 Mean < Median < Mode
Long left tail		 \( S < 0 \)

\[ S = \frac{n}{(n-1)(n-2)} \sum \left( \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{s} \right)^3 \] 		Test scores in an easy exam, retirement ages, lifespans of electronic devices.
Case Study: In a university exam, most students score very high, but a few outliers with very low scores pull the mean down.

Types of Kurtosis ⛰️

Type Description Formula Example
Leptokurtic 🔼 Highly peaked, indicating heavy tails \[ K = \frac{\sum (x - \mu)^4}{N\sigma^4} - 3 \] Fat tails (e.g., financial crises data, stock market crashes, medical expenses in rare diseases).
Case Study: During a stock market crash, most stocks see normal returns, but extreme losses and gains create heavy tails.
Mesokurtic 🏔️ Normal distribution with moderate tails \[ K = 0 \] Moderate tails (e.g., standardized test scores, height distribution of adults, IQ scores).
Case Study: Standardized test scores (like SAT scores) show a normal distribution with most students scoring around the average.
Platykurtic ⬇️ Flat distribution with thin tails \[ K < 0 \] Thin tails (e.g., uniform distribution, rolling a fair die, random lottery numbers).
Case Study: The results of rolling a fair six-sided die form a uniform distribution where no extreme values occur frequently.
